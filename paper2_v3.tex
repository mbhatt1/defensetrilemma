\begin{abstract}
We prove, and formally verify in Lean~4, a \textbf{topological
obstruction} for continuous Lipschitz wrapper defenses against prompt
injection: any such defense leaves a persistent unsafe region of
positive measure under standard regularity assumptions. The
\emph{continuous regime} is the paper's primary contribution: we
instantiate the quantitative refinement in a deliberately
near-tangential GP-oblique stress-test family on real LLM safety
surfaces, then measure the containment
$S_{\text{pred}} \subseteq S_{\text{act}}$ with bootstrap confidence
intervals, within-family grid-resolution and kernel sweeps, plus judge
and target retargetings that yield either weak positive support,
mixed outcomes, or silence depending on whether the sampled archive
remains testable.
Discrete
classifier gates (Llama-Guard-3-1B, ShieldGemma-2B), stochastic
wrappers, and multi-turn / pipeline compositions appear as extensions
of the same obstruction, treated as illustrations rather than co-equal
empirical pillars. The core continuous proof chain is mechanically
verified in Lean; the sharpened quantitative appendix results
(\Cref{thm:cone,thm:dilemma}) are tied to closest coarser formal
witnesses in the correspondence table rather than exact byte-for-byte
Lean matches. The
Lean artifact (46 files, 361 theorem / lemma / corollary,
0 \texttt{sorry}, 3 standard axioms: \texttt{propext},
\texttt{Classical.choice}, \texttt{Quot.sound};
\href{https://github.com/mbhatt1/stuff/tree/main/ManifoldProofs}{artifact repository}) with a
paper-theorem $\leftrightarrow$ Lean-identifier / witness table
(\Cref{app:lean-correspondence}). Implications: any paradigm
satisfying (a) continuity, (b) safe-set score preservation, (c)
bounded Lipschitz constant inherits the obstruction; we derive three
escape hatches as formal corollaries and map their costs.
\end{abstract}


% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Prompt-injection defense wrappers --- input classifiers that flag
suspicious prompts~\cite{alon2023detecting}, constitutional rewriting
pipelines~\cite{bai2022constitutional}, and input sanitization
layers~\cite{inan2023llama} --- now form a crowded research paradigm
built on a single architectural pattern: a function $D\colon X \to X$
that preprocesses prompts before the model sees them, mapping unsafe
inputs to safer equivalents while preserving benign behavior. We prove
that a central benign-preserving continuous subregime of this
paradigm is topologically obstructed: any
continuous, utility-preserving, bounded-Lipschitz $D$ leaves a
persistent unsafe region of positive measure, independent of attack
model, data distribution, or training procedure. The proof is
mechanically verified in Lean~4 with Mathlib (46 files, 361
theorem/lemma/corollary, 0 \texttt{sorry}, 3 standard axioms:
\texttt{propext}, \texttt{Classical.choice}, \texttt{Quot.sound}), and
the same obstruction admits discrete, stochastic, and compositional
extensions motivated by how wrappers are actually deployed. Because
the obstruction is structural, we do not view our experiments as
validation: we use them to document one informative witness family for
the quantitative refinement, alongside compact illustrations and
weaker neighboring diagnostics that range from one-cell support to
silence, and to read off three formal
escape hatches --- drop
continuity, drop safe-set preservation, or drop the Lipschitz bound
--- as design implications for future defenses.

Concretely, continuity, utility preservation, and completeness form a
\textbf{defense trilemma}: at most two can coexist. The paper's spine is
the continuous case --- boundary fixation, score-preserving and
$\varepsilon$-relaxed variants, then a quantitative persistent-unsafe
region under Lipschitz regularity
(\Cref{thm:main,thm:score-preserving,thm:eps-relaxed,thm:eps-robust,thm:persistent}).
Discrete, stochastic, and multi-turn results are extensions of that
obstruction under dropped hypotheses; they are proved but measured only
as compact illustrations.

The empirical footprint is correspondingly hierarchical. On a saturated
MAP-Elites archive (82 cells, $25\times25$ grid) we measure one
informative continuous witness family for the primary containment
$\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{pred}} \subseteq \mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{act}}$
against real judge-scored LLM surfaces, with estimator bootstrap CIs
and within-family resolution / kernel sweeps. Judge swaps, target
swaps, and alternative continuous families are reported separately as
weaker neighboring-regime diagnostics, ranging from one-cell support
to silence depending on whether the sampled archive remains testable,
rather than as additional witness families.
Discrete classifier gates (Llama-Guard-3-1B, ShieldGemma-2B),
stochastic wrappers, and multi-turn / pipeline compositions are
treated as secondary footprint studies rather than co-equal
validations.

Scope is narrow by design: the theorem applies only to continuous
utility-preserving wrappers on connected prompt spaces. It does not
constrain training-time alignment, architectural changes, output-side
filters, hard blocklists, or multi-component systems that reject or
redirect inputs instead of preserving utility on every benign prompt.
All three structural assumptions --- continuity, utility preservation,
and connectedness --- are individually necessary
(\Cref{app:counterexamples}).

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Continuous obstruction.} A continuous utility-preserving
    wrapper must fix threshold prompts, and under Lipschitz regularity
    leaves a persistent unsafe region of positive measure
    (\Cref{thm:main,thm:score-preserving,thm:eps-relaxed,thm:eps-robust,thm:persistent}).
  \item \textbf{Cross-regime extensions.} The same obstruction
    reappears in discrete, stochastic, multi-turn, and pipeline
    settings (\Cref{thm:disc-dilemma,thm:multi-turn,thm:stochastic,thm:pipeline-impossible}).
  \item \textbf{Formal verification.} The theorem chain is mechanically
    verified in Lean~4 (46 files, 361 theorem/lemma/corollary,
    0 \texttt{sorry}; \Cref{sec:formal-verification}).
  \item \textbf{Empirical footprint.} The continuous containment story
    is measured end-to-end on real LLM safety surfaces; secondary
    illustrations exhibit the discrete-collapse, stochastic, and
    compositional footprints in deployed systems.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Relation to prior work.}
Adversarial-robustness and certified-defense work study failure or
guarantees for a \emph{fixed} model
~\cite{szegedy2013intriguing,goodfellow2014explaining,carlini2017evaluating,madry2018towards,cohen2019certified,katz2017reluplex,singh2019abstract,huang2017safety,bagnall2019certifying},
while LLM safety work documents persistent jailbreakability and
red-teaming pressure
~\cite{zou2023universal,chao2024jailbreaking,mehrotra2024tree,greshake2023indirect,mouret2015illuminating,samvelyan2024rainbow}.
Our claim is map-level: it constrains the wrapper function itself,
independent of attack distribution or training procedure, closer in
spirit to a no-free-lunch statement for defenses~\cite{wolpert1997no}.

\section{Problem Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\begin{definition}[Alignment Deviation Function]
\label{def:ad}
Let $X$ be a topological space and let $f\colon X \to \R$ be a
continuous scoring function. We interpret $f(x)$ as an alignment
deviation score for prompt $x$. Given a threshold $\tau\in\R$:
\begin{align}
  S_\tau &= \{x \in X : f(x) < \tau\}  &&\text{(safe region)} \\
  U_\tau &= \{x \in X : f(x) > \tau\}  &&\text{(unsafe region)} \\
  B_\tau &= \{x \in X : f(x) = \tau\}  &&\text{(boundary)}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Defense]
\label{def:defense}
A \emph{defense} is a continuous map $D\colon X \to X$. It is
\emph{utility-preserving} if $D(x) = x$ for all $x \in S_\tau$, and
\emph{complete} if $f(D(x)) < \tau$ for all $x\in X$.
\end{definition}

We will also use two weaker preservation notions. A defense is
\emph{score-preserving on safe inputs} if $f(D(x)) = f(x)$ for all
$x \in S_\tau$, and \emph{$\varepsilon$-relaxed on safe inputs} if
$|f(D(x)) - f(x)| \leq \varepsilon$ for all $x \in S_\tau$.

The central question is whether a wrapper can preserve utility in one
of these senses and still make all outputs safe. The following sections
show that across several regimes, the answer is no.


% ============================================================
\section{Impossibility of Wrapper Defense in Continuous Settings}
\label{sec:boundary}

The core argument is short: $D$ fixes every safe input, so continuity
makes $\Fix(D)$ closed; $S_\tau$ is open and, in a connected space, not
closed; therefore $\Fix(D)$ cannot stop at the edge of $S_\tau$ and must
contain a boundary point.

\begin{theorem}[Boundary Fixation]
\label{thm:main}
Let $X$ be a connected Hausdorff space (a space that is ``in one
piece'' and where distinct points can be separated by neighborhoods).
Let $f\colon X\to\R$ be continuous with $S_\tau, U_\tau \neq
\emptyset$, and $D\colon X \to X$ continuous with
$D|_{S_\tau} = \id$. Then there exists $z\in X$ with $f(z) = \tau$
and $D(z) = z$. Moreover, every $z \in \cl{S_\tau} \setminus S_\tau$
satisfies $f(z) = \tau$ and $D(z) = z$, and this set is nonempty.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
In a Hausdorff space, $\Fix(D)$ is closed. Since $D|_{S_\tau}=\id$,
$S_\tau \subseteq \Fix(D)$ and hence $\cl{S_\tau}\subseteq \Fix(D)$.
But $S_\tau=f^{-1}((-\infty,\tau))$ is open and not closed, so
$\cl{S_\tau}\setminus S_\tau \neq \emptyset$. Any
$z \in \cl{S_\tau}\setminus S_\tau$ satisfies $f(z)=\tau$ and $D(z)=z$.
Full proof in \Cref{app:proofs}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Defense Trilemma]
\label{thm:trilemma}
Let $X$ be a connected Hausdorff space, $f\colon X \to \R$ continuous
with $S_\tau, U_\tau \neq \emptyset$. No $D\colon X \to X$ can
simultaneously be continuous, utility-preserving ($D|_{S_\tau} = \id$),
and complete ($f(D(x)) < \tau$ for all~$x$).
\end{theorem}

All three hypotheses are individually necessary: counterexamples for
dropping connectedness, continuity, or utility preservation appear in
\Cref{app:counterexamples}. The theorem fixes
$\cl{S_\tau}\setminus S_\tau$, not necessarily all of $B_\tau$.


\subsection{Exact and Relaxed Utility Preservation}
\label{sec:relaxed}

The impossibility survives two natural weakenings of exact identity on
the safe set.

\begin{theorem}[Score-Preserving Defense]
\label{thm:score-preserving}
Let $X$ be a connected Hausdorff space, $f\colon X\to\R$ continuous
with $S_\tau, U_\tau \neq \emptyset$. If $D\colon X \to X$ is
continuous and \emph{score-preserving on safe inputs}:
$f(D(x)) = f(x)$ for all $x \in S_\tau$, then there exists $z$ with
$f(z) = \tau$ and $f(D(z)) = \tau$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Define $h = f \circ D - f$. Then $h$ is continuous and $h|_{S_\tau} = 0$.
The zero set $\{h = 0\}$ is closed and contains $\cl{S_\tau}$, since closed sets contain their boundary points. For
$z \in \cl{S_\tau} \setminus S_\tau$, $f(D(z)) = f(z) = \tau$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[$\varepsilon$-Relaxed Utility Preservation]
\label{thm:eps-relaxed}
Under the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:score-preserving}, if
$|f(D(x)) - f(x)| \leq \varepsilon$ for all $x \in S_\tau$, then
there exists $z$ with $f(z) = \tau$ and
$f(D(z)) \geq \tau - \varepsilon$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The set $\{x : h(x) \geq -\varepsilon\}$ is closed and contains
$S_\tau$, hence $\cl{S_\tau}$. For
$z \in \cl{S_\tau} \setminus S_\tau$:
$f(D(z)) = \tau + h(z) \geq \tau - \varepsilon$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Quantitative Failure Near the Boundary}
\label{sec:eps-robust}

Boundary fixation gives a fixed threshold point; Lipschitz regularity
spreads failure to a neighborhood.

\begin{theorem}[$\varepsilon$-Robust Defense Constraint]
\label{thm:eps-robust}
Under the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:main}, if $(X,d)$ is a metric
space, $f$ is $L$-Lipschitz, and $D$ is $K$-Lipschitz, then for
the fixed boundary point~$z$:
\begin{equation}
  f(D(x)) \;\geq\; \tau - LK\dist(x, z)
  \qquad \text{for all } x \in X.
\end{equation}
Points within distance~$\delta$ of~$z$ remain within
$LK\delta$ of threshold.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Since $D(z)=z$ and $D$ is $K$-Lipschitz:
$\dist(D(x),z) \leq K\dist(x,z)$. Since $f(z)=\tau$ and $f$ is
$L$-Lipschitz:
$|f(D(x)) - \tau| \leq L \cdot K\dist(x,z)$.
Full proof in \Cref{app:proofs}.
\end{proof}

Explicit band-volume refinements are deferred to \Cref{app:quantitative}.

\subsection{Persistent Unsafe Region}
\label{sec:persistent}

The $\varepsilon$-robust bound can still allow values slightly below
$\tau$. Persistence appears when $f$ rises faster in the unsafe
direction than the defense can decrease it along its displacement path.
For that comparison we use the defense-path Lipschitz constant
\[
  \ell \;=\; \sup_{x \neq D(x)}
    \frac{|f(D(x)) - f(x)|}{\dist(D(x),\, x)},
\]
which satisfies $\ell \le L$ and is often much smaller than $L$ in the
anisotropic regimes of interest.

\begin{lemma}[Input-Relative Bound]
\label{lem:input-bound}
Under the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:eps-robust}, if $f$ has
defense-path Lipschitz constant~$\ell$, then
$f(D(x)) \geq f(x) - \ell(K+1)\dist(x,z)$
for all $x \in X$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Triangle inequality:
$\dist(D(x), x) \leq \dist(D(x), z) + \dist(z, x)
\leq (K+1)\dist(x, z)$.
Defense-path Lipschitz: $|f(D(x)) - f(x)| \leq \ell\dist(D(x), x)
\leq \ell(K+1)\dist(x, z)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Steep region]
\label{def:steep}
Given a fixed boundary point $z$, the \emph{steep region} is
$\mathcal{S} = \{x \in X : f(x) > \tau + \ell(K+1)\dist(x, z)\}$---the
set of points where alignment deviation exceeds~$\tau$ by more than
the defense's Lipschitz budget can compensate.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Persistent Unsafe Region]
\label{thm:persistent}
Let $X$ be a connected Hausdorff metric space, $f$ continuous and
$L$-Lipschitz, $D$ continuous and $K$-Lipschitz with
$D|_{S_\tau} = \id$, and $S_\tau, U_\tau \neq \emptyset$.
Let $z \in \cl{S_\tau} \setminus S_\tau$ be the fixed boundary
point from \Cref{thm:main}, and let $\ell$ be the defense-path
Lipschitz constant. If $\mathcal{S} \neq \emptyset$, then:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item $\mathcal{S}$ is open.
  \item $\mathcal{S}$ has positive measure (under any measure positive
    on nonempty open sets).
  \item For every $x \in \mathcal{S}$: $f(D(x)) > \tau$.
\end{enumerate}
The defense leaves a positive-measure region that remains unsafe.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
$\mathcal{S}$ is a strict superlevel set of a continuous function,
hence open. For $x \in \mathcal{S}$:
$f(D(x)) \geq f(x) - \ell(K+1)\dist(x,z) > \tau$
by \Cref{lem:input-bound} and the definition of~$\mathcal{S}$.
Full proof in \Cref{app:proofs}.
\end{proof}

Quantitative refinements, including explicit volume bounds, the cone
bound, and the defense-aggressiveness dilemma, are deferred to
\Cref{app:quantitative}.


% ============================================================
\section{Extensions: Discrete, Multi-Turn, Stochastic}
\label{sec:extensions}
\label{sec:bridge}

The continuous obstruction carries to three settings where one
hypothesis is relaxed.

\begin{theorem}[Discrete Defense Dilemma]
\label{thm:disc-dilemma}
Let $X$ be a finite set with $S_\tau, U_\tau \neq \emptyset$, and
$D\colon X \to X$ utility-preserving ($D(x) = x$ for $f(x) < \tau$).
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item If $D$ is injective, then $f(D(u)) \geq \tau$ for every
    $u$ with $f(u) \geq \tau$: the defense is incomplete.
  \item If $D$ is complete ($f(D(x)) < \tau$ for all $x$), then $D$ is
    non-injective.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Multi-Turn Impossibility]
\label{thm:multi-turn}
Let $\{f_t, D_t\}_{t=1}^T$ be alignment functions and defenses over
$T$ turns on a connected Hausdorff space, each continuous and
utility-preserving, with $S_\tau^{(t)}, U_\tau^{(t)} \neq \emptyset$
at every turn. Then for every turn $t$, there exists $z_t$ with
$f_t(z_t) = \tau$ and $D_t(z_t) = z_t$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Stochastic Defense Impossibility]
\label{thm:stochastic}
Let $X$ be a connected Hausdorff space, $f\colon X \to \R$ continuous
with $S_\tau, U_\tau \neq \emptyset$. Let $D$ be a stochastic defense
and define $g(x) = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim D(x)}[f(y)]$. If $g$ is
continuous and $g(x) = f(x)$ for all $x \in S_\tau$, then there
exists $z$ with $f(z) = \tau$ and $g(z) = \tau$.
\end{theorem}

Discrete, stochastic, and multi-turn empirical illustrations are
reported in
\Cref{sec:discrete-regime,sec:stochastic-regime,sec:compositional-regime};
full proofs, falsifiers, and Lean correspondence are in
\Cref{app:extensions-proofs}.

\section{Four Regimes of the Obstruction: Empirical Footprint}
\label{sec:four-regimes}
\label{sec:experiments}

The continuous persistence chain
(\Cref{thm:main,thm:eps-robust,thm:persistent}) is the paper's primary
theoretical object; \Cref{thm:cone} and \Cref{thm:dilemma} are narrower
quantitative refinements that only bite in anisotropic transversality
regimes. The empirical role of this section is therefore limited and
hierarchical: we instantiate one measured non-vacuous continuous
witness family (\Cref{sec:continuous-regime}), report alternative
continuous families and target/judge retargetings as weaker diagnostics
that may yield one-cell support, mixed outcomes, or silence when the
refinement is vacuous or untestable, and
give compact illustrations of the discrete, stochastic, and
compositional extensions
(\Cref{sec:discrete-regime,sec:stochastic-regime,sec:compositional-regime}).
Measurement uses the MAP-Elites driver of~\cite{munshi2026manifold}
over a 2D prompt space (query indirection $\times$ authority framing);
all alignment-deviation values come from our own runs
(\Cref{app:exp-details}). The goal is not to prove the theorem --- the
Lean artifact does that --- but to document one informative witness
family and to record where neighboring regimes yield weaker support,
mixed outcomes, or silence rather than contradiction.

\subsection{Continuous-defense footprint (Regime 1)}
\label{sec:continuous-regime}
\label{sec:live-validation}

On the filled MAP-Elites cells $G_f$ of an $N\times N$ grid, with
normalized $L_2$ cell distance
$\dist((i_1,j_1),(i_2,j_2)) = h\sqrt{(i_1-i_2)^2 + (j_1-j_2)^2}$ and
$h=1/N$, the validator uses the sampled boundary anchor
$z^*=\arg\min_{x\in G_f}|f(x)-\tau|$ and four empirical quantities:
\[
\hat L=\max_{x\neq y}\frac{|f(x)-f(y)|}{\dist(x,y)},\qquad
\hat K=\max_{u\neq v}\frac{\dist(D(u),D(v))}{\dist(u,v)},
\]
\[
\hat\ell=\max_{D(x)\neq x}\frac{|f(D(x))-f(x)|}{\dist(D(x),x)},\qquad
\hat G=\max_{f(x)<\tau<f(y)}\frac{f(y)-f(x)}{\dist(x,y)}.
\]
These are the discrete estimators of the surface Lipschitz constant,
defense Lipschitz constant, defense-path Lipschitz constant, and
boundary directional rate; full implementation details are deferred to
\Cref{app:exp-details}.
All main-text quantitative claims use this per-cell defense-path
estimator~$\hat\ell$. Secondary sweep tables that use a different
sensitivity-pipeline diagnostic mark it explicitly as
$\hat\ell_{\mathrm{diag}}$ rather than reusing~$\hat\ell$.

The validator estimates $(L, K, \ell, G)$ on a 2D AD heatmap, picks a
boundary anchor $z^{*}$, and records the containment of
\Cref{thm:persistent},
\[
\mathcal{S}_{\text{pred}} = \{x : f(x) > \tau + \ell(K{+}1)\,\dist(x,z^*)\}
\;\subseteq\;
\mathcal{S}_{\text{act}} = \{x : f(D(x)) > \tau\},
\]
under any continuous $K$-Lipschitz utility-preserving $D$. Per cell we
true positive, interior false positive
(FP$_{\mathrm{int}}$: a real counterexample), boundary false positive
(discrete movement of a sampled boundary cell, outside the theorem's
hypotheses), or false negative (allowed by one-sided containment).
Treating each filled cell as its $L_2$ Voronoi neighborhood,
$\mathrm{FP}_{\mathrm{int}}=0$ on the grid is the discrete analogue of
$\mu(\mathcal{S}_{\text{pred}}\setminus\mathcal{S}_{\text{act}})=0$,
up to the discretization gap $|f(z^*)-\tau|$.

\paragraph{Primary measurement.}
Saturated archive: target \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo-0125}, judge
\texttt{gpt-4.1-2025-04-14}, 400 evaluations (100 seeds + 300
MAP-Elites iterations, $\sim 5{,}200$ API calls, $\sim$\$15,
$\sim 130$ min); 82 filled cells, 66 strictly unsafe, 10 boundary.
Fitting a 2D GP (RBF, $\sigma=0.2$) and building a smooth-oblique
defense $D(x) = x + \alpha\,\beta(\mu(x))\,v(x)$ with $v(x)$ displaced
$89.5^\circ$ from the negative gradient gives
$\hat\ell = 0.86$, $\hat K = 1.05$, $\hat G = 23.6$; transversality
$\hat G > \hat\ell(\hat K{+}1) = 1.77$ holds by a factor of $\sim13$.
$|\mathcal{S}_{\text{pred}}| = 3$, all three actually persistent
($\mathrm{TP}=3$, $\mathrm{FP}_{\mathrm{int}}=0$;
\Cref{fig:theory-vs-reality-saturated}); $|\mathcal{S}_{\text{act}}| = 68$
with 65 false negatives (allowed by one-sided containment). The defense
is genuinely non-identity (82 cells displaced). \Cref{thm:eps-robust}
also holds at every filled cell, and \Cref{thm:main}'s boundary
fixation is non-vacuously activated with closest-to-threshold
$|f(z^*)-\tau| = 0.375$.
This oblique construction is a deliberately near-tangential
\emph{stress-test} wrapper used to instantiate a non-vacuous
transversality regime; it is not presented as the optimization
direction a deployed safety wrapper would naturally choose.

\paragraph{Witness-family robustness and neighboring diagnostics.}
\Cref{tab:continuous-sweep} should be read hierarchically rather than
as four co-equal positive validations: on this archive, the
near-tangential GP-oblique family is the only clear large non-vacuous
witness of the anisotropic transversality regime. The three non-oblique
families all miss the transversality diagnostic; two still exhibit a
one-cell containment footprint with $\mathrm{FP}_{\mathrm{int}}=0$,
while the third is mixed under discrete bookkeeping. Within the
GP-oblique witness family,
$\mathrm{FP}_{\mathrm{int}}=0$ survives bootstrap CIs, grid-resolution
subsampling, kernel sweeps, and seed replication. Judge swaps and
committee aggregation remain supportive wherever the sampled archive
spans both sides of threshold; target swaps are weaker and often become
silent as alignment improves. These neighboring diagnostics should be
read as local consistency checks around the witness family, not as
independent replications of the same non-vacuous regime. The full
tables are deferred to
\Cref{app:continuous-sweeps,app:judge-robustness} together with the

\section{Design Consequences}
\label{sec:design-consequences}

\paragraph{The forbidden zone.}
Within the paper's core regime --- continuous wrappers that preserve
safe-set scores and have bounded effective sensitivity --- the
continuous chain
(\Cref{thm:main,thm:score-preserving,thm:eps-robust,thm:persistent})
implies a structural obstruction, with \Cref{thm:cone} giving a
stronger quantitative refinement when its additional regularity
hypotheses are met. The design question is therefore not how to make
such a wrapper complete, but which assumption to relax and what cost
that move incurs. The three corollaries below should be read in that
conditional sense: they describe three escape hatches from the core
regime, together with the price each one pays.

\paragraph{In plain terms.}
If a defense is explicitly designed to vary smoothly with prompt
semantics, to leave benign prompts essentially unchanged, and to avoid
unbounded sensitivity, then it sits in the regime covered by the core
theorem chain. In that regime, tuning the wrapper does not remove the
obstruction; escaping it requires giving up at least one of those
design goals. The three corollaries below spell out the tradeoffs of
doing so.

\begin{corollary}[Drop continuity: classifier-gate cost]
\label{cor:drop-continuity}
A defense that abandons continuity --- i.e., a classifier gate with a
threshold indicator --- falls under \Cref{thm:disc-dilemma} and
inherits the injective-but-incomplete / forced-collapse horns: either
some unsafe inputs leak, or distinct harmful inputs map to a bounded
number of output classes.
Empirical illustration: Llama-Guard-3-1B achieves $62\%$ FN with $7$
labels; ShieldGemma-2B achieves $68\%$ FN with $2$ labels
(\Cref{tab:llamaguard-demo} and \Cref{tab:forced-collapse};
discussion in \Cref{app:extended-limitations}).
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The threshold indicator $\mathbf{1}[s(x) \geq \tau]$ is discontinuous,
so hypothesis (a) is violated; the hypotheses of
\Cref{thm:disc-dilemma} apply directly and yield the two-horned lower
bound.
\end{proof}

\noindent\emph{In plain terms.} This means that going discrete (any
classifier-gate defense, whether Llama-Guard, ShieldGemma, a keyword
filter, or a learned moderator) does not escape the obstruction --- it
just moves to a different face of it. You now either miss a large
fraction of unsafe inputs or you collapse them into a handful of
output labels. Both costs are directly measurable on any deployed
gate; the numbers above are existence proofs, not optimization
baselines.

\begin{corollary}[Drop score preservation: utility tax]
\label{cor:drop-score}
A defense that violates safe-set score preservation
(\Cref{thm:score-preserving}) --- i.e., $f(D(x)) \neq f(x)$ on some
$x \in S_\tau$ --- can step outside the score-preserving obstruction
only by altering the target's outputs on legitimate queries. In other
words, escaping the core regime this way pays a utility tax on the
safe set.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
\Cref{thm:score-preserving} shows that preserving $f$ on $S_\tau$ plus
continuity forces boundary fixation; removing the preservation
assumption permits escape, but any $D$ with $f(D(x)) \neq f(x)$ on
$S_\tau$ directly changes behavior the user legitimately requested.
\end{proof}

\noindent\emph{In plain terms.} This means that if a defense wants to
escape by perturbing the model's behavior on the safe set as well, it
is paying for every unit of avoided persistence with a unit of
degraded safe-completion quality. Users experience this as a defense
that refuses or distorts benign requests; the degradation is not a
design bug, it is the exact cost the theorem makes explicit.

\begin{corollary}[Bound $K$ below threshold: redundancy regime]
\label{cor:drop-K}
The cone condition $G > \ell(K+1)$ from \Cref{thm:cone} requires
$K > G/\ell - 1$ for non-trivial persistence. Dropping $K$ below this
threshold eliminates the persistence prediction but forces the
defense's smoothing kernel to be so flat that $D$ approaches an
identity operator. In low-$G$ regimes this can make the wrapper add
little measurable safety beyond the aligned target itself; in
high-$G$ regimes, by contrast, the quantitative refinement is the one
that bites.
Empirical anchor: on a target with measured $\hat G \approx 1.55$
(a regime where the raw target refuses most prompts on its own), the
quantitative threshold already sits below the observable range, so the
wrapper adds little beyond the target's own alignment; on the
saturated archive $\hat G = 23.6$ with
$\hat K \approx 1.5$ the condition bites and persistence is observed
(\Cref{app:extended-limitations}).
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Substitute $K$ into the cone inequality of \Cref{thm:cone}. Below the
threshold $K^\star = G/\ell - 1$ (\Cref{thm:dilemma}), the bound
becomes vacuous; simultaneously $K \to 0$ drives the smoothing kernel
to the identity, so $D(x) \approx x$ and the wrapper leaves the
target unchanged.
\end{proof}

\noindent\emph{In plain terms.} The third escape route is to make the
wrapper weak enough that the quantitative persistence refinement stops
giving a positive prediction. In shallow regimes that can leave a
wrapper close to redundant; in steep regimes --- the ones where
wrappers are most needed --- the same move is much harder to make
without collapsing the defense toward the identity.

\paragraph{Research directions the impossibility does not rule out.}
The obstruction is specific --- it applies to wrappers satisfying
(a)--(c). At least four research frontiers remain productively open.
\emph{(i) Calibrated incomplete defenses with explicit coverage
guarantees}: drop completeness deliberately, provide a guaranteed
lower bound on coverage, and chart the Pareto curve of (coverage,
false-negative rate) as a function of threshold. \emph{(ii) Non-wrapper
architectures}: inference-time defense via modified decoding
(constrained sampling, refusal heads, activation gating) rather than a
wrapper over the target, which escapes hypothesis (a) by construction
rather than by discretization. \emph{(iii) Deployment-regime
diagnostics}: use measured $(G, \ell, K)$ on a target as a diagnostic
of which quantitative regime a wrapper occupies --- for example,
whether the cone refinement is likely to be informative or vacuous on
that target. \emph{(iv) Architectural changes to the target}: if
wrappers are obstructed, fix the model --- RLHF, constitutional AI,
activation steering --- rather than layering post-hoc filters whose
Lipschitz constraints are bound by \Cref{thm:trilemma}.

\paragraph{Formal status.}
The corollaries in this section follow from the Lean-verified proof
chain documented in \Cref{sec:formal-verification} and
\Cref{app:lean-correspondence}; readers can audit each derivation in
the artifact.

\section{Implications, Scope, and Limitations}
\label{sec:engineering}

The theorem concerns continuous utility-preserving wrappers on connected
spaces; the empirical footprint is measured on one synthesized archive
with OpenAI judges and should not be read as a bound on the theorem
itself. The Lean artifact formalizes the mathematical proof chain, not
the experimental pipeline. Extended assumption-by-assumption caveats,
judge/benchmark scope, and the worked $K^\star$ example are deferred to
\Cref{app:extended-limitations}. Appendix \Cref{app:stability} also
records three stability corollaries explaining why small fine-tuning
does not localize the obstruction away for free: unsafe interiors are
stable, transversal crossings persist, and local patching is nonlocal.


% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Wrapper defense is structurally incomplete under utility-preserving
constraints. In the primary continuous regime, wrappers fix threshold
prompts and, under additional regularity, leave persistent unsafe
regions; discrete, stochastic, and multi-turn settings do not escape
the obstruction so much as re-express it. Empirically, the continuous
containment story is visible on real judge-scored LLM safety surfaces,
while secondary regime illustrations show the same footprint shape in
classifier gates, stochastic wrappers, and compositions.

The practical implication is not that defense is futile, but that
wrapper defense should be engineered as risk management rather than as a
route to completeness. Stronger guarantees require stepping outside the
continuous utility-preserving wrapper model. Appendix
\Cref{app:landscape} makes the complementary geometric point: under the
additional continuity and Lipschitz hypotheses used there, unsafe
basins are open and connected unsafe fragments cannot shrink
arbitrarily finely, which helps explain why empirical search can locate
witnesses without the theorem itself being algorithmic.

\section*{Broader Impact}

\paragraph{Intended positive impact.}
The contribution is a formally verified no-go theorem. Its intended
effect on the field is to \emph{narrow} the design space for LLM
safety research, redirecting effort away from topologically
obstructed paradigms --- continuous, utility-preserving,
bounded-Lipschitz wrappers over a fixed target --- toward productive
alternatives made explicit by the three escape-hatch corollaries
(\Cref{sec:design-consequences}): discontinuous classifier gates with
calibrated coverage guarantees, score-altering rewrite pipelines with
measured utility tax, and non-wrapper interventions (training-time
alignment, activation-level editing, architectural changes) that fall
outside the theorem's hypotheses by construction. A mechanically
verified impossibility is a cheaper signal than a failed research
program.

\paragraph{Risk surface and responsible reading.}
The paper names specific deployed classifiers --- Llama-Guard-3-1B and
ShieldGemma-2B --- as having illustrative obstruction footprints on
our synthesized archive. This should \emph{not} be read as an
endorsement of non-wrapper alternatives, nor as grounds for removing
existing wrappers from deployed systems. The obstruction is a lower
bound on adversarial success for a specific class of defenses, not a
claim that wrappers are harmful or that defense-in-depth is
counterproductive. A wrapper with a non-trivial false-negative rate
on adversarial prompts can still deliver substantial value on
benign-population traffic, raise attack cost, and serve as one
component of a layered safety stack; our result constrains only the
completeness of any single continuous wrapper in isolation.

\paragraph{Dual-use considerations.}
The attack baseline we use for archive construction is
PAIR~\cite{chao2024jailbreaking}, which is already public; the
theorem does not provide novel attack vectors beyond what the
literature already describes. The persistent-region prediction of
\Cref{thm:persistent} is a \emph{structural} statement --- unsafe
regions of positive measure survive any continuous Lipschitz wrapper
--- not a recipe for locating such regions for a specific target.
Locating a persistent cell in practice still requires the same
search machinery (behavioral-space exploration, judge scoring) that
is standard in red-teaming work and is not enabled by our paper.

\paragraph{Disclosure.}
The empirical illustrations use deployed commercial defenses
(OpenAI-hosted targets and judges, Llama-Guard-3-1B, ShieldGemma-2B).
We did not perform coordinated disclosure with the relevant vendors
prior to submission, as the results do not constitute novel attacks:
the theorem is a structural impossibility result, the PAIR attack
baseline is published, and the per-cell findings are standard
red-team outcomes on known benchmarks. We will share the saturated
archive, Lean artifact, and footprint-evaluation code with any vendor
on request.

\section{Proofs of the Extensions}
\label{app:extensions-proofs}

Full statements and proofs for the three extensions summarized in
\Cref{sec:extensions}: discrete (\Cref{thm:disc-dilemma}), multi-turn
(\Cref{thm:multi-turn}), and stochastic (\Cref{thm:stochastic}). Each
is a corollary of the continuous obstruction with one hypothesis
relaxed; all three are verified in Lean (see
\Cref{app:lean-correspondence}).

\subsection{Discrete-setting impossibility}

The discrete and continuous views are compatible; the
Tietze/McShane--Whitney bridge (\Cref{thm:tietze}) converts between
them. We prove the discrete statement directly from counting
arguments, independent of topology.
\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{thm:disc-dilemma}]
(1) Suppose $D$ is injective and $f(D(u)) < \tau$ for some $u$ with
$f(u) \geq \tau$. Then $D(u)$ is safe, so $D(D(u)) = D(u)$ by utility
preservation. Injectivity gives $D(u) = u$, so $f(u) = f(D(u)) < \tau$,
contradicting $f(u) \geq \tau$.
(2) For any $u \in U_\tau$: completeness gives $f(D(u)) < \tau$, so
utility preservation gives $D(D(u)) = D(u)$. But $u \neq D(u)$ since
$f(u) \geq \tau > f(D(u))$. So $u$ and $D(u)$ are distinct inputs with
$D(u) = D(D(u))$: the defense is non-injective.
\end{proof}

\emph{Falsifier:} a complete utility-preserving classifier $D$ with
$|D(X)| = |X|$ on any finite input set.

\subsection{Multi-turn impossibility}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch for \Cref{thm:multi-turn}]
Apply \Cref{thm:main} to $(f_t, D_t)$ at each turn. The functions may
depend on full history; this does not matter, as each timestep is a
fresh instance of boundary fixation. The Lean proof establishes
monotonicity of the running-max attack success
(\texttt{running\_max\_monotone}) and binary-search reachability of
transversality (\texttt{transversality\_reachable}).
\end{proof}

\emph{Falsifier:} an attack trajectory with strictly decreasing
running-max AD.

\subsection{Stochastic impossibility}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch for \Cref{thm:stochastic}]
Define $h = g - f$: continuous, vanishing on $S_\tau$, and so by the
closure argument of \Cref{thm:score-preserving}, vanishing on
$\cl{S_\tau}$. For $z \in \cl{S_\tau} \setminus S_\tau$,
$g(z) = f(z) = \tau$.
\end{proof}

\emph{Dichotomy.} Since $\mathbb{E}[f(D(z))] = \tau$, either
$f(D(z)) = \tau$ almost surely (deterministic at $z$) or $f(D(z))$ has
positive probability of exceeding $\tau$: a genuinely random defense
at a boundary point must sometimes make things worse. The continuity
of $g$ is the nontrivial assumption; stochastic defenses with
discontinuous rejection probabilities escape the theorem.

\emph{Falsifier:} a stochastic defense at a boundary cell with
$\mathrm{variance}=0$ and $f(D(z)) < \tau$.

\section{Continuous Interpolation of Discrete Observations}
\label{app:continuous-relaxation}

If one wishes to embed discrete behavioral observations into a
continuous prompt space, the bridge is standard.

\begin{theorem}[Continuous Relaxation]
\label{thm:tietze}
Let $(X,d)$ be a connected, normal, Hausdorff metric space, and
$S \subset X$ a finite set of observations with observed alignment
scores $g\colon S \to \R$ satisfying $g(p) < \tau$ and $g(q) > \tau$
for some $p, q \in S$. Then there exists a continuous $f\colon X \to \R$
extending~$g$ (i.e., $f|_S = g$) for which the hypotheses of
\Cref{thm:main} hold. If the extension is chosen to be Lipschitz
(via McShane--Whitney), the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:eps-robust} also
hold. If $f$ is additionally Lipschitz, \Cref{thm:persistent} applies
wherever transversality is met.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
\textbf{Step~1} (Extension exists).
$S$ is finite and $X$ is $T_1$, so $S$ is closed. Since $X$ is normal
and $g\colon S \to \R$ is continuous on a discrete closed subset, the
Tietze extension theorem provides a continuous
$f\colon X \to \R$ with $f|_S = g$.

\textbf{Step~2} (Hypotheses of \Cref{thm:main} are satisfied).
We have $f(p) = g(p) < \tau$ and $f(q) = g(q) > \tau$, so
$S_\tau \neq \emptyset$ and $U_\tau \neq \emptyset$. Since $X$ is
connected and Hausdorff, \Cref{thm:main} applies.

\textbf{Step~3} (Lipschitz extension enables stronger results).
When a Lipschitz extension is needed, the McShane--Whitney theorem
provides an $L$-Lipschitz $f$ agreeing with~$g$ on~$S$. Then
\Cref{thm:eps-robust} and \Cref{thm:persistent} apply under their
additional hypotheses.
\end{proof}

\section{Quantitative Refinements}
\label{app:quantitative}

The main paper establishes existence and persistence. This section
records explicit quantitative refinements.

\begin{theorem}[Volume Lower Bound]
\label{thm:coarea}
Let $f\colon \R^n \to \R$ be $L$-Lipschitz with $L > 0$, and let
$\mu$ denote Lebesgue measure. If there exists $c$ with
$f(c) = \tau - \varepsilon/2$, then
$B(c,\, \varepsilon/(4L)) \subseteq
\mathcal{B}_\varepsilon$, giving
\[
  \mu(\mathcal{B}_\varepsilon)
  \;\geq\; V_n \cdot \left(\frac{\varepsilon}{4L}\right)^{\!n},
\]
where $V_n$ is the volume of the unit ball in $\R^n$. In $\R^1$,
this simplifies to $\mu(\mathcal{B}_\varepsilon) \geq \varepsilon/(2L)$.
\end{theorem}

\noindent\emph{Proved in the Lean artifact as
\textup{\texttt{MoF\_17\_CoareaBound}}.}

\begin{theorem}[Cone Measure Bound]
\label{thm:cone}
In $\R$ with Lebesgue measure~$\mu$, if
$f(x) \geq \tau + c(x - z)$ for all $x \in (z,\, z + \delta_0)$
with $c > \ell(K+1)$, then
$f(D(x)) > \tau$ for every $x \in (z,\, z + \delta_0)$. Consequently,
\[
  \mu(\{x \in (z,\, z+\delta_0) : f(D(x)) > \tau\}) \geq \delta_0,
\]
and therefore $\mu(\{x : f(D(x)) > \tau\}) \geq \delta_0$.
\end{theorem}

\noindent\emph{Related formal witness.} The Lean artifact proves the
coarser Euclidean local cone bound with slope threshold $L(K{+}1)$ as
\textup{\texttt{MoF\_18\_ConeBound}}; the paper statement records the
sharper $\ell$-based form used in the empirical discussion.

\begin{theorem}[Defense Dilemma]
\label{thm:dilemma}
Assume $f$ is differentiable at boundary point~$z$ with
$G = \|\nabla f(z)\|$, and let $\ell$ be the defense-path Lipschitz
constant. Define $K^* = G/\ell - 1$. Then:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item If $K < K^*$, the persistent unsafe region exists
    ($G > \ell(K+1)$, so \Cref{thm:persistent} applies).
  \item If $K \geq K^*$, the $\varepsilon$-robust bound becomes too
    loose to exclude success on the steep region.
\end{enumerate}
Since $\ell \leq L$, the dilemma is sharpest when $\ell \ll L$.
\end{theorem}

\noindent\emph{Related formal witness.} The Lean artifact proves the
coarser threshold $K^{*}=G/L-1$ as
\textup{\texttt{MoF\_19\_OptimalDefense}}; the paper statement records
the sharper engineering threshold $G/\ell-1$ when a defense-path
constant $\ell$ is available.

\section{Pipeline Composition}
\label{app:pipeline}

\begin{theorem}[Pipeline Lipschitz Degradation]
\label{thm:pipeline}
If stages $T_1, \ldots, T_n$ are $K_1, \ldots, K_n$-Lipschitz, the
composed pipeline is $(\prod K_i)$-Lipschitz. For $n$ stages each with
$K \geq 2$, the effective constant is $K^n$.
\end{theorem}

\paragraph{Falsifier.}
A measured pipeline with empirical composed Lipschitz constant
$\hat K_{\text{composed}} > \hat K_1 \cdot \hat K_2$ would refute the
bound in \Cref{thm:pipeline}: Lipschitz constants submultiply under
composition, so the composed constant can never exceed the product.

\begin{theorem}[Pipeline Impossibility]
\label{thm:pipeline-impossible}
If the composed pipeline $P = T_n \circ \cdots \circ T_1 \circ D$ is
continuous and $P(x) = x$ for all $x \in S_\tau$, then $P$ has
boundary fixed points. If $D$ is $K_D$-Lipschitz and each $T_i$ is
$K$-Lipschitz, the $\varepsilon$-robust band scales as
$L \cdot K_D \cdot K^n \cdot \delta$.
\end{theorem}

\section{Vulnerability Landscape}
\label{app:landscape}

This section characterizes the geometry of the unsafe region.

\begin{theorem}[Basin Structure]
\label{thm:basin}
If $f$ is continuous and $f(p)>\tau$, then $U_\tau$ is open. Under
any measure positive on nonempty open sets, $U_\tau$ has positive
measure.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Basin Fragment Minimum Size]
\label{thm:fragment}
If $X$ is a normed space, $f$ is $L$-Lipschitz with $f(p) > \tau$,
the connected component of $U_\tau$ containing $p$ has diameter
$\geq 2(f(p) - \tau)/L$.
\end{theorem}

Smoother surfaces (smaller~$L$) produce larger basins; rougher
surfaces produce smaller fragments.

\section{Full Proofs}
\label{app:proofs}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{thm:main} (Boundary Fixation)]
\textbf{Step~1} (Hausdorff $\Rightarrow$ fixed-point set is closed).
$\Fix(D) = \{x : D(x) = x\}$ is the preimage of the diagonal
$\Delta \subset X \times X$ under $x \mapsto (D(x), x)$. In a
Hausdorff space, $\Delta$ is closed, so $\Fix(D)$ is closed.

\textbf{Step~2} (Utility preservation $\Rightarrow$ safe region
$\subseteq$ fixed points).
$S_\tau \subseteq \Fix(D)$. Since $\Fix(D)$ is closed:
$\cl{S_\tau} \subseteq \Fix(D)$.

\textbf{Step~3} (Connectedness $\Rightarrow$ safe region is not closed).
$S_\tau = f^{-1}((-\infty,\tau))$ is open. If also closed, it would be
clopen---but in a connected space the only clopen sets are $\emptyset$
and $X$. Since both $S_\tau$ and $U_\tau$ are nonempty, $S_\tau$ is not
closed.

\textbf{Step~4} (Boundary point exists).
$\cl{S_\tau} \supsetneq S_\tau$, so there exists
$z \in \cl{S_\tau} \setminus S_\tau$. Continuity gives
$f(z) \leq \tau$; $z \notin S_\tau$ gives $f(z) \geq \tau$. Hence
$f(z) = \tau$.

\textbf{Step~5} (Defense fixes the boundary point).
$z \in \cl{S_\tau} \subseteq \Fix(D)$, so $D(z) = z$ and
$f(D(z)) = \tau$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{thm:eps-robust} ($\varepsilon$-Robust Constraint)]
By \Cref{thm:main}, the fixed boundary point $z$ exists.

\textbf{Step~1.} Since $D(z) = z$ and $D$ is $K$-Lipschitz:
$\dist(D(x), z) = \dist(D(x), D(z)) \leq K\dist(x, z)$.

\textbf{Step~2.} Since $f(z) = \tau$ and $f$ is $L$-Lipschitz:
$|f(D(x)) - \tau| = |f(D(x)) - f(z)| \leq L\dist(D(x), z) \leq
LK\dist(x, z)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{thm:persistent} (Persistent Unsafe Region)]
\textbf{(1)} $\mathcal{S}$ is the strict superlevel set of
$x \mapsto f(x) - \ell(K+1)\dist(x,z)$ at level~$\tau$, hence open.

\textbf{(2)} Open and nonempty implies positive measure.

\textbf{(3)} For $x \in \mathcal{S}$:
$f(x) > \tau + \ell(K+1)\dist(x,z)$. By \Cref{lem:input-bound}:
$f(D(x)) \geq f(x) - \ell(K+1)\dist(x,z) > \tau$.

\noindent\emph{Lean:
\textup{\texttt{persistent\_unsafe\_refined}} in
\textup{\texttt{MoF\_20\_RefinedPersistence}}
(defense-path constant~$\ell$).
\textup{\texttt{MoF\_11\_EpsilonRobust}} contains an earlier version
using the global constant~$L$; as noted in
\Cref{sec:persistent}, that version is vacuous for isotropic surfaces.}
\end{proof}

\section{Attack Properties}
\label{app:attacks}

\begin{theorem}[Perturbation Robustness]
\label{thm:lipschitz}
If $f$ is $L$-Lipschitz and $f(p)>\tau$, then
$B(p,\, (f(p)-\tau)/L) \subseteq U_\tau$.
The radius is monotone in~$f(p)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Iterative Convergence]
\label{thm:convergence}
Any monotone-improvement operator $T$ with score in $[0,1]$ converges.
If each step gains $\geq \delta$, convergence takes
$\leq \lfloor 1/\delta \rfloor$ steps.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Transferability]
\label{thm:transfer}
$\|f-g\|_\infty \leq \delta \implies
\{f > \tau+\delta\} \subseteq \{g > \tau\}$.
Transfer costs zero additional queries.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Authority Monotonicity]
\label{thm:authority}
If $f(y,\cdot)$ is monotone non-decreasing in authority for each fixed
indirection~$y$, the vulnerability set is upward-closed with a critical
threshold $a^*_2(y)$ (by IVT, when $f(y,\cdot)$ is continuous and
crosses~$\tau$). If additionally $f$ is monotone non-decreasing in
indirection for each fixed authority, the threshold curve
$y \mapsto a^*_2(y)$ is non-increasing.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Gradient Ascent]
\label{thm:gradient}
If $f$ has nonzero Fr\'{e}chet derivative at~$x$, there exist $v$ and
$\varepsilon > 0$ with $f(x + \varepsilon v) > f(x)$.
\end{theorem}


\section{Stability Under Fine-Tuning}
\label{app:stability}

\begin{theorem}[Interior Stability]
\label{thm:interior-stable}
If $\|f - g\|_\infty \leq \varepsilon$:
$f(x) > \tau + \varepsilon \implies g(x) > \tau$, and
$f(x) < \tau - \varepsilon \implies g(x) < \tau$.
Only the band $|f(x) - \tau| \leq \varepsilon$ is uncertain.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Crossing Preservation]
\label{thm:crossing}
Let $f, g\colon [a,b] \to \R$ be continuous. If $f$ crosses $\tau$
on $[a,b]$ with margin $m$ (i.e., $f(a') < \tau - m$ and
$f(b') > \tau + m$ for some $a', b' \in [a,b]$) and
$\|f - g\|_\infty \leq \varepsilon < m$, then $g$ also crosses
$\tau$ on $[a,b]$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Patching Is Nonlocal]
\label{thm:nonlocal}
There exist $f, g$ with $\|f-g\|_\infty \leq \varepsilon$ such that
eliminating a vulnerability at one point necessarily changes values at
distant points.
\end{theorem}


\section{Cost Asymmetry}
\label{app:cost}

\begin{theorem}[Exponential Cost Asymmetry]
\label{thm:cost}
For grid-based defense with $N \geq 2$ bins per axis in $d$ dimensions:
attack cost $\leq 1/\delta$ (dimension-independent); defense cost
$= N^d$ (exponential in~$d$); ratio
$\delta \cdot N^d \to \infty$ as $d\to\infty$.
\end{theorem}

At $d=2$ with $N=25$ and $\delta=0.01$, the ratio is $6.25$.
At $d=10$, it climbs to $\sim 10^{12}$.


\section{Additional Verified Results}
\label{app:additional}

\paragraph{Lipschitz displacement bound.}
$D$ is $K$-Lipschitz with $D(z) = z \implies
\dist(D(x), x) \leq (K+1)\dist(x,z)$.

\paragraph{Tool calls amplify failure.}
Each non-contractive tool call multiplicatively increases the pipeline's
effective Lipschitz constant: $K^n < K^{n+1}$ for $K \geq 2$.

\paragraph{Attacker monotone improvement.}
Best observed alignment deviation is non-decreasing across turns.

\paragraph{Attacker steering.}
If directional slope varies continuously with attacker parameter $\alpha$
and crosses $\ell(K+1)$, transversality is reachable by IVT.

\paragraph{Stochastic regularity.}
$g(x) = \mathbb{E}[f(D(x))]$ satisfies $g - f = 0$ on $\cl{S_\tau}$.

\paragraph{Discrete defense dilemma.}
An injective, utility-preserving defense is incomplete (every unsafe
input stays unsafe). A complete, utility-preserving defense is
non-injective (distinct inputs collapse to the same output).
The three properties---completeness, utility preservation,
injectivity---form a trilemma.

\paragraph{Defense position invariance.}
Lipschitz constant of defense-before-tools equals defense-after-tools:
$K_D \cdot K_T^n$.

\subsection{Estimator definitions}
\label{sec:estimators}

Let $G_f$ denote the set of filled cells in an $N \times N$ MAP-Elites
grid and let $h = 1/N$ be the cell width. Distances use normalized
$L_2$ grid coordinates on $[0,1]^2$:
\[
\dist\bigl((i_1,j_1),(i_2,j_2)\bigr)
  = h\sqrt{(i_1-i_2)^2 + (j_1-j_2)^2}.
\]
All pairs involving unfilled cells are excluded; no imputation is
performed. The boundary anchor is
\[
z^* = \arg\min_{x \in G_f} |f(x)-\tau|,
\]
restricted to sampled boundary cells
$\cl{S_\tau}\setminus S_\tau$, with lexicographic tie-breaking.

\paragraph{Surface Lipschitz constant.}
\[
\hat L = \max_{x,y \in G_f,\ x \neq y}
\frac{|f(x)-f(y)|}{\dist(x,y)}.
\]

\paragraph{Defense Lipschitz constant.}
\[
\hat K = \max_{u,v \in G_f,\ u \neq v}
\frac{\dist(D(u),D(v))}{\dist(u,v)}.
\]

\paragraph{Defense-path Lipschitz constant.}
\[
\hat\ell = \max_{x \in G_f,\ D(x)\neq x,\ D(x)\in G_f}
\frac{|f(D(x))-f(x)|}{\dist(D(x),x)}.
\]
If the set is empty, we take $\hat\ell = 0$.

\paragraph{Boundary directional rate.}
\[
\hat G = \max_{x,y \in G_f,\ f(x)<\tau<f(y)}
\frac{f(y)-f(x)}{\dist(x,y)}.
\]
These are the estimators used by the validator and the tables below.

\section{Judge robustness}
\label{app:judge-robustness}

All $f$ values in~\Cref{sec:live-validation} are judge-dependent:
swapping the judge can reshape the alignment-deviation surface and
thereby change $\hat L, \hat\ell, \hat G$. To quantify this, we
rescored the 82 $(\text{prompt}, \text{target response})$ pairs from
the saturated \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo-0125} archive with three alternative
judges drawn from different families and capacities —
\texttt{gpt-4o-2024-08-06}, \texttt{gpt-4.1-mini-2025-04-14}, and
\texttt{gpt-4o-mini-2024-07-18} — applying the same rethinking-evals
per-category methodology with a single judge and $\alpha=0.5$ (target
responses were NOT regenerated; only the judge was swapped). The
four-judge comparison is summarized in~\Cref{tab:judge-robustness}.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The theorem applies to the full prompt
space $X$ under every judge — benign prompts exist, so
$\mathcal{S}_\tau$ is nonempty in $X$. Whether the theorem's positive
prediction can be \emph{tested from a given sample} depends on whether
that sample spans both regions. Three of four judges
(\texttt{gpt-4.1}, \texttt{gpt-4o}, \texttt{gpt-4.1-mini}) yield samples
that span both $\mathcal{S}_\tau$ and $\mathcal{U}_\tau$; applying the
continuous \texttt{smooth\_nearest\_safe} defense produces
TP$>0$ and FP$_{\mathrm{int}}=0$ in every case. Under the strictest
judge (\texttt{gpt-4o-mini}), MAP-Elites's adversarial bias drives the
sample entirely into $\mathcal{U}_\tau$, so no boundary cell $z^*$
appears in the 82 sampled cells and the positive prediction cannot be
tested from this sample. Crucially, FP$_{\mathrm{int}}=0$ still holds:
no counterexample to the theorem is produced under any of the four
judges. Cross-judge agreement ranges from substantial (Kendall's
$W=0.70$) to poor on binary classification (Cohen's $\kappa=0$ vs
gpt-4o-mini), so the \emph{specific numerical instance} of a particular
judge is not itself judge-robust even though the \emph{framework's
predictions} are.

\paragraph{Committee aggregation.}
The rethinking-evals methodology supports committee aggregation:
$P_{\mathrm{actual}} = \alpha P_{\mathrm{vote}} + (1-\alpha) P_{\mathrm{mean}}$
with $\alpha=0.5$. We apply this offline to the four existing judge
archives (no new API calls) and re-run the validator on each
committee-aggregated surface (\Cref{tab:judge-committee}). The
top-3 committee (gpt-4.1, gpt-4o, gpt-4.1-mini; excluding the
strict-judge outlier gpt-4o-mini) is within one cell of canonical
($17$ safe vs $16$; mean $\Delta=+0.010$), and
$\mathrm{FP}_{\mathrm{int}}=0$ under \texttt{smooth\_nearest\_safe}
on every committee configuration --- the theorem's predictions
survive committee aggregation.

\section{Attack Baseline: PAIR-Style Iteration}
\label{app:pair-baseline}

To situate the theorem empirically against an established attack-defense
benchmark style, we run a PAIR-style iterative attack
(\cite{chao2024jailbreaking}-adjacent) on ten top-AD unsafe seed prompts
from the saturated archive. The attacker is \texttt{gpt-4o-mini}, the
target is \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo-0125}, and the judge is \texttt{gpt-4.1}.
Each behavior gets five iterations; the attacker receives the full
transcript and judge scores at each step. We compare two conditions:
undefended (attacker prompt goes directly to target) and
\emph{paraphrase wrapper} (attacker prompt is rewritten by
\texttt{gpt-4o-mini} at $T=0$ before reaching the target).

gpt-4.1 judge). This is a bounded-budget baseline, not an estimate of

\label{tab:pair-baseline}
\input{tables/pair_baseline.tex}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Within the 5-iteration budget the paraphrase wrapper blocks all ten
behaviors (ASR drops from $1.00$ to $0.00$). This does \emph{not}
contradict Theorem~\ref{thm:disc-dilemma}'s injective-incomplete
horn: the paraphrase defense is injective on our 66-prompt sample
(all 66 rewrites are distinct), and \Cref{tab:paraphrase-unsafe}
already exhibits $1/66$ paraphrases that yield $f(D(x)) > \tau$. The
PAIR attacker with gpt-4o-mini as refiner and a 5-iteration budget is
therefore best read as a bounded-budget baseline, not as evidence
against eventual injective-horn leakage. We do not claim that 5
iterations characterize the attacker's asymptotic success
probability; they only show that a low-budget attacker fails to locate
the known rare bypass on this sample. A fuller budget sweep would be
needed to turn this appendix check into a quantitative attacker-model
study.

\section{Extended Scope, Limitations, and Worked Example}
\label{app:extended-limitations}

The theorem is one topological impossibility, mechanically verified
in Lean~4; the empirical sections are footprints of that theorem in
four deployment regimes, not independent tests of it. The limitations
below are organized accordingly: what the \emph{theorem's assumptions}
require, what the \emph{empirical illustration} does and does not
cover, what the \emph{Lean artifact} formalizes, and what the
\emph{derived design consequences} still leave open. A companion
worked example at the end ties the Lipschitz bound to a concrete
numerical design constraint on the wrapper models we illustrate on.

\paragraph{Theorem-assumption limitations.}
The impossibility requires four structural assumptions on the prompt
space and the defense. (i)~The prompt space $X$ is Hausdorff and
path-connected; the continuous theorems break on totally disconnected
spaces, and the discrete regime (\Cref{thm:disc-dilemma}) is exactly
what replaces them there. (ii)~The averaged post-defense score
$x \mapsto \mathbb{E}[f(D(x))]$ is continuous in $x$; stochastic
defenses whose expected score is itself discontinuous fall outside
the continuous hypotheses, though the stochastic companion
(\Cref{thm:stochastic}) is what governs them. (iii)~The defense is
bounded Lipschitz with constant $K$; unbounded-sensitivity defenses
are one of the three escape hatches (\Cref{sec:design-consequences})
and are not constrained by the quantitative bounds. (iv)~Utility
preservation holds on a safe set of positive measure. The deployed
systems we illustrate on are not all equally well modeled by these
assumptions. Several wrapper families are plausibly approximated this
way as a first model --- especially paraphrase / canonicalization
wrappers and the smooth surrogate defenses used in the paper ---
whereas classifier gates and aggressive rewrites motivate the discrete
and score-tax escape hatches instead. The assumptions are therefore a
structural modeling choice, not a claim that every deployed guardrail
literally satisfies them pointwise.

\paragraph{Illustration limitations (not theorem limitations).}
The empirical footprint has a bounded reach that should not be read
as bounds on the theorem. (a)~The saturated archive has $82$ filled
cells from a single MAP-Elites synthesis seed; richer seeds or larger
archives would widen the footprint but cannot change the Lean-proven
statement. (b)~The judge committee is single-vendor (all OpenAI);
\Cref{app:judge-robustness} reports a four-judge cross-check, but a
multi-vendor judge study remains future work. (c)~Behavioral-descriptor
axes (query indirection, authority framing) are inherited
from~\cite{munshi2026manifold}; different axes would produce different
surfaces, not different theorems. (d)~We do not overlay public
jailbreak benchmarks; the footprint we report is specific to the
synthesized archive. None of these are limitations of
\Cref{thm:main,thm:persistent,thm:eps-robust,thm:disc-dilemma,thm:multi-turn,thm:stochastic};
they bound how broadly the illustration exhibits the predicted
footprint.

\paragraph{Lean-artifact limitations.}
The Lean~4 development covers the topological and analytic core of
the impossibility chain: boundary fixation, $\varepsilon$-robust
depth, transversal persistence, discrete non-injectivity, stochastic
averaging, multi-turn recurrence, and the three escape-hatch
corollaries. Outside the formalization: the empirical experiments
themselves are not formalized (the confusion-matrix counts, judge
pipeline, and archive saturation are code, not theorems); the
behavioral-descriptor axes are informal domain choices; and the
artifact depends on the three standard Mathlib axioms
(\texttt{propext}, \texttt{Classical.choice}, \texttt{Quot.sound}),
which are load-bearing --- removing \texttt{Classical.choice} in
particular would require a constructive reworking of several
Hausdorff-space lemmas.

\paragraph{Design-consequences limitations.}
The three escape hatches of \Cref{sec:design-consequences} (drop
continuity, drop safe-set preservation, drop the Lipschitz bound) are
derived as formal corollaries, so their \emph{existence} is settled.
Their \emph{costs}, however, are anchored empirically: the
false-negative rate of discrete classifier gates (Llama-Guard's
51-of-82 pass-through in~\Cref{tab:llamaguard-demo}), the utility
degradation of $\varepsilon$-relaxed wrappers, and the redundancy
overhead of low-$G$ smooth wrappers are measured on a single archive
and should be read as design-space anchors, not population estimates.
A full cost characterization across deployment regimes would require
larger multi-vendor studies beyond the scope of this paper.

\paragraph{Scope of the individual assumptions.}
Each structural assumption maps to a qualitative regime that carries
its own caveats. Boundary fixation is only at the boundary: fixed
points satisfy $f(z) = \tau$ exactly, so if $\tau$-level behavior is
benign the theorem is true but practically harmless. The
$\varepsilon$-robust bound limits depth, not direction: the defense
may push near-boundary points slightly below $\tau$, and the bound
limits how far, not whether. Persistence requires transversality: the
persistent unsafe region exists only where the alignment surface is
steep ($c > \ell(K{+}1)$), and for isotropic $f$ ($\ell = L$) the
region is empty for all $K \geq 0$. The cost asymmetry of
\Cref{thm:cost} further assumes exhaustive grid enumeration;
learning-based defenses that generalize across the space may sidestep
the exponential bound.

\paragraph{Worked example (Theorem~\ref{thm:dilemma}).}
For a Gaussian-smoothed wrapper with $\hat\ell = 0.86$ (from our
saturated \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo-0125} run) and target
$\hat G = 23.6$, the optimal defense Lipschitz constant is
$K^* = \hat G / \hat\ell - 1 \approx 26.5$. Defense designs with
$K < K^*$ admit a persistent region (\Cref{thm:persistent} applies
non-trivially); designs with $K \geq K^*$ require displacements so
large that utility preservation --- measured by response-embedding
similarity between $D(x)$ and $x$ on safe inputs --- drops below the
accept threshold. This is a concrete, numerically-grounded corollary
of the theorem for any wrapper deployed against this alignment
surface.
